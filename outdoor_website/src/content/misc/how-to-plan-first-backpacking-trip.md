## Start Smaller Than You Think

Most people pick too ambitious a route their first time. A 2-night, 15-mile loop on a well-marked trail is enough to figure out what you're doing. Save the remote wilderness for year two.

Good criteria for a first trip:
- Well-marked trail (no navigation required beyond following blazes)
- Under 8 miles per day
- Cell service or at least a popular enough trail that you'll see other hikers
- Established campsites with water nearby

## The Permit Question

Many popular trails now require permits, especially in Colorado. Check Recreation.gov before you commit to a route. Some permits (like Rocky Mountain National Park wilderness permits) release months in advance; others have walk-up availability.

For your first trip, an unpermitted national forest trail is often the easiest entry point — less planning friction, more flexibility.

## The Gear You Actually Need

You don't need to buy everything at once. Borrow what you can, rent what you can't borrow, and only buy the things you'll definitely use again.

**The Big Three (where money matters):**
- **Shelter** — a 2-person tent in the 3–5 lb range is fine for a beginner
- **Sleep system** — sleeping bag rated to 20°F covers most 3-season Colorado nights
- **Pack** — 50–65L for 2–3 nights; fit matters more than brand

**Everything else (borrow or buy cheap for now):**
- Headlamp
- Trekking poles (optional but reduce knee stress significantly on descents)
- Water filter (Sawyer Squeeze — see our review)
- Stove + fuel canister
- First aid kit

## Food Planning

Use the [Trail Provisions calorie calculator](/backpacking-calorie-calculator) to estimate how much food to bring. The common mistake is packing too much on a first trip out of anxiety — and then suffering under the weight.

For a 2-night trip at moderate activity, most people need:
- 1.75–2 lbs of food per day
- Target 100 cal/oz to keep weight reasonable
- No-cook lunches save stove fuel and time

## The Night Before

- Pre-pack everything and weigh your loaded pack. Over 30 lbs? Look for cuts.
- Check the forecast. Afternoon thunderstorms are common in Colorado from July–September — plan to be below treeline by noon.
- Tell someone where you're going and when to expect you back.

## On Trail

- Set camp before 4pm
- Filter water before you need it, not when you're desperate
- Keep food in a bear canister or hang it — don't leave it in your tent

Pay attention to what bothered you. That's what you fix for the next one.
